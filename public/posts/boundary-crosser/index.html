Every Saint Was First a Scandal — Kabir. Bulleh Shah. Ibn Arabi. Nusrat.

They crossed the hardest boundary in their tradition. Got rejected, accused, cut off. Then they died — and everyone claimed them.

How every tradition eventually enshrines the person it once tried to erase — and what it quietly removes in the process.

There is a pattern so consistent across eight hundred years of South Asian and Islamic spiritual history that it stops feeling like coincidence and starts feeling like a law.

A person crosses the hardest available boundary in their tradition. During their lifetime: accused, rejected, called dangerous, called a heretic, called a sellout — or simply cut off by family, community, and institution. Then they die. Then everyone claims them.

The more completely they crossed the boundary, the more completely they get claimed afterward.

Kabir. Bulleh Shah. Ibn Arabi. Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan. The cases span Muslim-born mystics teaching under Hindu masters, Syed-born poets choosing low-caste teachers, Andalusian Sufis accused of heresy by the greatest orthodox theologians of their era, and a shrine musician from Lyallpur whose voice ended up in a Scorsese film. Different centuries, different geographies, different boundary types. Same pattern.

This is not a feel-good story about how genius is always recognized eventually. It is a story about how institutions handle disruption — and the specific cost of surviving long enough to be absorbed.

The Cases#

Kabir (c. 1398–1518) was born into a Muslim weaver family in Varanasi, in circumstances that are disputed and possibly mythologized. He became the disciple of Ramananda, a Vaishnava Hindu teacher. Both Hindu and Muslim authorities found this arrangement threatening. A Muslim who took Hindu initiation was apostasy from one side; a teacher who accepted Muslim students was social transgression from the other.

The legend of his death captures the pattern with strange precision. When Kabir died in Maghar — a place considered inauspicious for dying — Hindus and Muslims came to fight over his corpse, each wanting to perform their respective last rites. According to tradition, when they lifted the shroud, they found only flowers. They divided the flowers and went their separate ways.

The legend is almost certainly not factual. But it describes something true about what followed. The claiming began immediately. Sikhs incorporated 541 of his verses into the Guru Granth Sahib. Hindus have the Kabirpanth sect, founded by his followers, which continues today. Muslims maintain their own lineage claims. Each tradition has a version of Kabir — and the versions don’t always agree with each other. The three major collections of his verses (Bijak, Guru Granth Sahib, and Kabir Granthavali) present sometimes-divergent texts. The claiming has already altered the archive.

Bulleh Shah (1680–1757) was born a Syed — claiming descent from the Prophet, one of the highest status designations in Muslim society. He chose as his spiritual teacher Shah Inayat Qadiri, an Arain. The Arain were a gardening and farming caste. In the social hierarchy of Mughal Punjab, a Syed sitting at the feet of an Arain for spiritual instruction was a scandal.

His family did cut him off. He is said to have spent months performing in the markets of Lahore dressed as a woman dancer, trying to win back his teacher’s attention after a falling out. The crossing was not theoretical. It was enacted in his body, in his clothes, in his choices about where to sit and who to call murshid.

His kafis — the poetry form he worked in — describe the interior experience of that crossing. Bullehia, ki jaana main kaun — Bulleh, how do I know who I am? The question isn’t rhetorical. He genuinely crossed to a place where the conventional identity markers — caste, religion, status — no longer organized reality.

His tomb in Kasur is visited by Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs. He is claimed by all three. The man who was cut off by his own family for choosing the wrong teacher is now everyone’s saint.

Ibn Arabi (1165–1240) was born in Murcia, in what is now Spain, and died in Damascus after a life that covered most of the known Islamic world. His central doctrine — wahdat al-wujud, the Unity of Being — held that existence is singular and divine, that what we perceive as multiplicity is divine self-disclosure taking different forms. The stone idol perceived dimly by the idol-worshipper and the divine truth perceived clearly by the Sufi are, in this view, the same thing. The difference is clarity of perception, not object.

Ibn Taymiyya (1263–1328), the most consequential orthodox theologian of the era and a figure whose influence on contemporary Salafi and Wahhabi thought is direct, wrote extensively against Ibn Arabi. He was accused of heresy by significant portions of the Islamic scholarly establishment.

He died with the title Shaykh al-Akbar — the Greatest Master. The Ottoman Empire, which found his universalism politically useful for governing a multi-religious imperial territory, became a major institutional sponsor of his thought. The rehabilitation was not purely spiritual. It required state power.

Today he is one of the most translated and studied Sufi thinkers in the world. The specific accusations that nearly destroyed his reputation survive mainly as footnotes to his triumph.

Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan (1948–1997) made a different kind of crossing. He didn’t cross between religions. He crossed between worlds: sacred and secular, East and West, shrine and stadium. When Peter Gabriel brought him to WOMAD in 1985, when he worked with Michael Brook on electronic ambient productions for the Real World label, when his voice appeared in The Last Temptation of Christ and Dead Man Walking, the reaction from within the traditional Qawwali community was not celebratory.

Taking shrine music — music that exists in the specific context of devotional gathering at a saint’s dargah, music that is part of sama, the listening practice of Sufi communities — and moving it to concert halls, to Western pop production, to Hollywood soundtracks, was read as commerce, as decontextualization, or at minimum as a fundamental misunderstanding of what the music was for.

He died in 1997, at 48, of kidney failure, having apparently declined to take the medical advice that might have extended his life by years. Whether this was self-destruction or simply a life operating at maximum intensity without maintenance is not something anyone can say with certainty. He left more than 125 studio albums and uncountable hours of live recordings.

By the time a decade had passed after his death, the discourse about selling out had essentially vanished. He is now universally acknowledged as the defining Qawwal of the twentieth century. The claiming was the fastest in this set.

The Mechanism#

Why does this keep happening?

The boundary-crosser poses a specific threat: they demonstrate that a particular boundary is arbitrary. Kabir’s existence — born Muslim, initiated by a Hindu teacher, reaching genuine spiritual depth — was evidence that the Hindu-Muslim religious boundary was not cosmically enforced. You could be both, or neither, and still access what was real.

This is intolerable to any institution whose authority depends on the boundary being absolute. The institution’s first response is always suppression: threat, rejection, social excommunication. Kabir gets both communities’ anger. Bulleh Shah gets his family cut off. Ibn Arabi gets the most powerful theologian of the era writing refutations. Nusrat gets the raised eyebrow from the traditional community.

Suppression tends to fail when the crosser has too much truth, too much genius, or — usually — both in combination. The work circulates despite the institution’s disapproval. Followers multiply. Suppressing someone who produces work like this eventually makes the institution look worse than tolerating them would have.

After death, the calculation changes. The person is gone. What remains is text — poetry, music, doctrine — which the institution can interpret, curate, and gradually domesticate. The most radical implications can be de-emphasized. The most threatening specific claims can be gently moved to the periphery of the discussion. What survives in institutional memory is the revered figure. What tends not to survive fully intact is the specific challenge.

This is a stable equilibrium. The institution gets the prestige of association with an undeniable figure. The crosser gets permanent reverence. The boundary — the thing that was actually challenged — often does not survive the claiming with its challenge intact.

How Disruption Transmits#

The cases in this library reveal a sub-pattern that becomes visible only when you look at the teachers, not just the students.

Ramananda — Kabir’s Hindu teacher — was himself a transgressor in a smaller key. He taught across caste lines, an unusual act for a Vaishnava teacher of his era. He did not cross the Hindu-Muslim boundary. But he crossed the boundary that was available to him. His student Kabir crossed the larger one.

Shah Inayat Qadiri — Bulleh Shah’s teacher, the Arain gardener — crossed the caste barrier within Sufi Islam. Being spiritually accomplished enough that a Syed’s son sought him out was itself a transgression of the expected order. His student Bulleh Shah crossed the religious boundary the tradition had maintained.

The principle: the disruptor is downstream of a disruptor. The disruption is transmitted through the pedagogical relationship before it circulates through the work.

This has a corollary that rarely gets examined: if you want to understand why a particular boundary-crosser appeared, look at their teacher. The teacher is almost always less famous, almost always more forgotten, almost always harder to document. They crossed something smaller. But they opened a door that their student walked through and blew open.

The Silk Road entries in this library show the same mechanism at institutional scale: the Barmakids enabled the great translation movement that transferred Greek philosophy into Arabic. The Sogdians carried paper west before anyone else understood what they were carrying. The intermediary and the enabler are almost always forgotten faster than the crosser they made possible.

What Gets Lost in the Claiming#

This is where the pattern becomes most important to understand precisely, because the claiming looks like justice and sometimes is, and sometimes is not.

When Ibn Arabi’s thought traveled from Andalusia through the Islamic world and eventually into the Sufi culture of Punjab, the nuances of wahdat al-wujud compressed. What arrived in popular Sufi culture was something like: “God is in everything.” This is roughly true. It is also much cruder than what he actually wrote — a fully developed metaphysical system with precise distinctions about wujud (existence), tashbih (immanence), tanzih (transcendence), and the specific epistemological claims about how different beings perceive the divine through different veils. The doctrine as philosophy did not travel intact. The doctrine as devotional orientation did. The claiming preserved the reverence and compressed the argument.

With Kabir, the selection is visible in the textual record. The Guru Granth Sahib includes Kabir’s verses that fit within a Sikh theological frame — verses emphasizing devotion to the formless divine (nirguna bhakti), which aligns with Sikh theology’s rejection of idol worship. The Kabir who is equally scathing about Hindu idol worship and Muslim prayer ritual — who turns the same razor on both with equal sharpness — is present in the Bijak, the text maintained by the Kabirpanth, and less present in the Guru Granth Sahib selection.

The millstone doha is worth quoting directly: Pahan puje Hari mile, to main pujun pahar / Ya se to chakki bhalī, pis khaye sansar — roughly: “If worshipping a stone gets you God, I’ll worship a mountain; at least the millstone grinds and feeds the world.” This is Kabir at his most aggressive. The critique of ritual formalism is the same whether the ritual is Hindu or Muslim. Both traditions claiming him have to quietly step around this Kabir, or render him palatable, or simply include his softer verses and cite them more frequently than this one.

Nusrat is the most contemporary case, and the selection process is still ongoing, which makes it possible to watch in real time.

The claiming has established a hierarchy: the shrine recordings are the “authentic” Nusrat. The live mehfil recordings — twelve-hour sessions at dargahs, the traditional Qawwali form — are the core of his legacy. The Real World albums are treated as interesting, important, historic, but slightly adjacent to the main body of work. The collaborations with Michael Brook (Mustt Mustt, 1990; Night Song, 1996) — the records that actually crossed the boundary, that put his voice into Western electronic ambient music, that reached listeners who had never heard Qawwali and would never visit a dargah — these are being gently repositioned as footnotes.

This is the irony worth sitting with. The recordings that the traditional Qawwali community was most uncomfortable with — the ones that enacted the boundary-crossing most completely — are the ones being de-emphasized in the claiming. The traditional community, which was most ambivalent about what he did, is now most emphatic about his greatness. What they are emphatic about is a Nusrat they are more comfortable with: the shrine Nusrat, the mehfil Nusrat, the one whose context remained sacred even as his voice became global. The Real World Nusrat — the one who actually crossed — is being archived rather than centered.

Recording technology means this domestication cannot be completed the way it was with Kabir. The Michael Brook albums exist. Anyone can listen. The claiming cannot substantially rewrite the work. But it can establish a hierarchy of which work counts most, and that hierarchy is being written now, while the people who were ambivalent are still alive to write it.

Abida: The Side Door#

Abida Parveen’s case is structurally different, and worth examining separately because the difference reveals something about strategy.

Kabir, Bulleh Shah, and Ibn Arabi all confronted their boundary directly. Kabir’s dohas are aggressive speech acts. Ibn Arabi’s doctrine forced the institutional question into explicit form: if God is in everything, what does that mean for the idol, for the Kaaba, for the boundary between believer and unbeliever? The confrontation was built into the work itself. You could not receive it without encountering the challenge.

Abida crossed the gender boundary in Sufi music — the last major boundary the tradition maintained. Women were present in early Sufi history, and female saints are embedded in the tradition’s founding myths, but by the time Qawwali consolidated as a performance form, it was male. The mehfil, the devotional gathering, the shrine performance — these were male spaces.

She did not try to enter the Qawwali mehfil through the front door. She found the kafi.

The kafi is a solo form. Bulleh Shah’s poetry, Waris Shah’s poetry — these travel as kafi, as sung poetry with a single voice and accompaniment, rather than as Qawwali, which requires the ensemble, the call-and-response, the male collective. The kafi had female saints historically embedded in it. It was the form where female authority had never been fully foreclosed. She became the supreme practitioner of the form where a woman could have full authority — and from that position, transformed what Sufi devotional music meant entirely.

She has performed in churches. A Pakistani Muslim woman, singing Sufi Punjabi devotional poetry in Christian sacred spaces. This is Ibn Arabi’s doctrine enacted in performance: the specific vessel — mosque, church — is not the source; the devotion that passes through it is. She is performing the crossing he described.

Whether she will be claimed in the same way — whether the claiming will begin after her death and will compress or domesticate what she did — is still open. She is 72, still performing. The claiming hasn’t started. But the trajectory is visible.

Nusrat as the Contemporary Test Case#

The speed of Nusrat’s rehabilitation is worth pausing on, because it compresses into fifteen years what took Kabir a generation and Ibn Arabi a century and a half.

Several things accelerated it.

Recording fixed the archive. Kabir’s verses circulated orally for generations before being written down. Each transmission introduced variation. The claiming could reshape the archive because the archive was itself unstable — different communities preserved different verses, and no one version was canonical. Nusrat’s recordings are fixed. Every studio album, every bootlegged mehfil recording — these are not going to change. The claiming cannot rewrite the work in the way Kabir’s was rewritten, only establish hierarchies of importance within it.

Global simultaneity. Kabir’s claiming happened sequentially: one tradition claimed him, then another, then a third. Nusrat’s happened in parallel. The Pakistani music community, the Indian film industry, the Western world-music world, the global streaming audience — each arrived at the conclusion that Nusrat was theirs at roughly the same time, without coordination. Consensus formed faster when it formed across multiple communities simultaneously rather than being transmitted from one to another.

“World music” provided a frame. The Western music world had a category ready for Nusrat — a legitimizing genre that was neither “sacred” (which would have kept him in a niche) nor “pop” (which would have been dismissive). “World music,” whatever its limitations as a concept, gave the Western claiming a name. It positioned him as a cultural peer rather than an exotic artifact. This accelerated the Western legitimization, which fed back into the global consensus.

What the claiming is producing, fifteen years in, is a Nusrat who matters primarily as a master of traditional form. The canonical Nusrat is becoming the shrine Nusrat. The innovative Nusrat — the one who sat with Michael Brook and made something new, the one who crossed the boundary that the tradition’s guardians found uncomfortable — is being preserved but not centered.

This is not yet complete. It may not be completed. The recordings resist it. But the direction is clear.

The Question the Pattern Raises#

If every major boundary-crosser gets claimed, and if the claiming always involves some softening of the specific challenge they posed, then what did the boundary-crossing actually accomplish?

The optimistic answer: it shifts the Overton window. The tradition that rejected Kabir now includes him in scripture. Including him required domesticating him — the Kabir in the Guru Granth Sahib is not the most aggressive Kabir — but the domesticated version still says things the tradition would not have said before Kabir. The boundary has moved, even if the specific challenge has been partially neutralized.

The pessimistic answer: the institution absorbs the disruption and remains structurally unchanged. Kabir is in the Guru Granth Sahib, and caste discrimination continues in communities that revere him. Ibn Arabi is Shaykh al-Akbar, and the Sufi dargahs that Wahhabi movements have destroyed in the last century were built on his theology. The claiming is a form of neutralization that leaves the underlying structure intact.

Both are probably true at different timescales. On a fifty-year timescale, the institution wins: it domesticates the crosser and continues. On a five-hundred-year timescale, the crosser’s work has usually moved the available boundaries in some direction, even if the specific challenge was softened.

The mechanism doesn’t promise justice. It promises incorporation — and incorporation, while better than erasure, is its own form of loss.

What We Don’t Know Yet#

Three gaps in this analysis are worth naming explicitly.

The women who crossed and weren’t claimed. Almost all the documented boundary-crossers in this library are men. The female saints — Bibi Pak Daman, Rabia al-Adawiyya, the unnamed women in the tradition’s margins — are poorly documented. Were there women who crossed major boundaries and were simply suppressed, whose work didn’t circulate, whose communities were destroyed? Or were the available boundaries structured differently for women, such that the crossings took different forms? Abida Parveen is the only living case where this question can be studied directly. The pattern with her is visible; the eventual claiming is still ahead.

The failed crossers. Every case in this analysis is a successful crossing — where the work survived, circulated, and was eventually claimed. But suppression sometimes works. There must be many who crossed a boundary and were simply destroyed: whose texts were burned, whose communities were scattered, who left no legacy. We cannot see them. The pattern is built entirely from surviving cases. The graveyard of failed boundary-crossers is invisible in this data.

The institutional enabler. The Ramananda-Kabir and Shah Inayat-Bulleh Shah patterns are in the record. What made those enablers possible? What institutional conditions allowed a Vaishnava teacher to accept Muslim students, or allowed an Arain’s spiritual authority to become visible enough that a Syed would seek him out? The enabler who makes the crosser possible is almost always forgotten faster than the crosser. Documenting them may require looking at the conditions that produce teachers who can cross — not just the students who do.

The pattern holds. Pay during life. Get claimed after death. The claiming selects. What gets kept is the figure. What gets quietly modified is the challenge.

Knowing this changes how to read the claiming. When a tradition says “he was always ours,” the right question is: which version of him? Which verses did they include? Which collaborations did they center? The selection is the argument.
