Your Brain Is Running a Drill: The Real Psychology Behind True Crime's Grip

The obvious framing is morbid entertainment. The real story is a psychological survival system — preparation, empathy, and the brain's ancient threat-processing machinery.
March 30, 2026 · 11 min · Gaurav Yadav

There’s a well-worn piece of social commentary about true crime fans: they’re either macabre thrill-seekers, empathy addicts, or women who’ve confused anxiety for entertainment. The genre gets dismissed as poison dressed up as a podcast — lurid, exploitative, probably bad for you. And honestly, some of it is.

But here’s what the research actually says: consuming true crime may be one of the more evolutionarily sensible things a modern human can do with a commute.

The real story isn’t about morbidity. It’s about preparation. It’s about a brain that evolved to survive a far more dangerous world, now running practice drills on the only threatening content available to it — which, in the modern era, happens to come with serial-killer episode arcs and a husband-and-wife hosting dynamic.

The Simulation Hypothesis#

Start with Coltan Scrivner, a researcher who has studied morbid curiosity at the University of Chicago and the Max Planck Institute. His core argument — developed across several papers and crystallized in his 2025 book Morbidly Curious: A Scientist Explains Why We Can’t Look Away — is that morbid curiosity is not a character flaw. It is a stable personality trait that is functionally distinct from sensation-seeking, and it likely exists for a reason.

The reason: simulation.

The hypothesis is that consuming threatening content — murder narratives, disaster films, accounts of violence — functions as a low-cost simulation of danger. It’s the cognitive equivalent of a fire drill. You don’t need to be in an actual burning building to learn the layout, rehearse the exits, and manage the panic response. You just need a sufficiently vivid simulation.

Scrivner draws the analogy to rough-and-tumble play in animals. Young mammals wrestle, chase, and pin each other not because they’ve lost their minds but because mock-combat is how they develop the motor skills and emotional regulation they’ll need for the real thing. The play looks like chaos. The function is practice. True crime, in this framing, is the cognitive equivalent: a structured encounter with threat that exercises threat-processing machinery without putting you in actual danger.

This has a corollary that sounds counterintuitive until you sit with it: the brains that attend most vigilantly to threatening information are not damaged or morbid. They’re well-calibrated. Scrivner’s research connects morbid curiosity to what evolutionary psychologists call the negativity bias — the fact that human attention is asymmetrically tuned toward negative stimuli. This isn’t a malfunction. It’s a feature from an era when missing a predator once was game over.

The COVID Test Case#

The simulation hypothesis would be interesting if it were purely theoretical. It becomes genuinely compelling when it makes testable predictions — and those predictions turn out to be true.

In a 2021 study published in Personality and Individual Differences (PMC7492010), Scrivner and colleagues tested 310 participants during the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic. The question was simple: did engagement with frightening fiction correlate with any measurable resilience or preparedness in the face of a real-world catastrophe?

The answer was yes, and the specificity of the finding matters.

Horror fans — people who’d been consuming frightening content all along — showed greater psychological resilience during the pandemic. But the fans who scored highest on preparedness were those who’d engaged specifically with so-called “prepper genres”: apocalyptic films, zombie narratives, alien-invasion stories. Not horror broadly, but the subgenre most structurally similar to a real pandemic scenario. The simulation had been thematically specific.

There was also a dynamic effect that’s harder to dismiss as coincidence: in the early weeks of COVID, morbidly curious individuals became dramatically more interested in pandemic films. Not less. Their consumption calibrated upward in response to a perceived novel threat, as though the brain had recognized a gap in its threat library and started filling it. That’s not a person using entertainment to escape reality. That’s a person using entertainment to prepare for one.

The study’s conclusion: “exposure to frightening fictions allow audiences to practice effective coping strategies that can be beneficial in real-world situations.”

Horror as rehearsal. True crime as a briefing.

The 73% Problem (Which Isn’t Actually a Problem)#

Any serious analysis of true crime has to grapple with the gender split. Women make up somewhere between 73 and 75 percent of the audience, depending on the study. This gets treated as a curiosity at best and, at worst, as evidence of something slightly embarrassing — that women are more easily hooked by melodrama, or more susceptible to fear-based content, or simply more prone to a particular flavor of anxious consumption.

The research suggests a different interpretation: women are the primary audience because the genre is, functionally, most useful to them.

A 2025 grounded theory analysis by White (published in The Journal of Integrated Studies at Athabasca University) analyzed how Reddit users in true crime communities describe their engagement with the genre. The study identified what White called a “Capacities for Empathy” schema — a cluster of four interrelated motivations:

Empathizing with victims. Not rubbernecking, but genuine identification. Understanding what happened to someone like you.

Perceiving risk for victimization. Recognizing patterns. Learning who, where, how.

Rehearsals of survival. Mentally walking through what you would do. This one deserves its own sentence: users in these communities describe explicitly practicing their responses — visualizing exits, rehearsing decisions, running the scenario. It is not passive consumption. It is active cognitive preparation.

Interpassive justice. Experiencing, vicariously, a world where accountability happens. This is the cathartic function, and while it matters, it’s the least evolutionarily interesting of the four.

Women are disproportionately victims of intimate interpersonal violence. This is not a contested statistic. If you are statistically more likely to be targeted by someone you know — in your home, by a partner, in the context of ordinary life — then understanding how it happens is not morbid. It’s adaptive. True crime is effectively teaching a population with elevated threat exposure about threat patterns.

A 2022 study by Clausen, Hargett, and Rivers at Winthrop University (n=105) found that women experienced true crime as more fear-inducing than men — and also more informational. The two responses weren’t in tension. Fear is part of the mechanism. If consuming true crime activates fear-processing and forces you to think through scenarios, then feeling afraid is part of the drill.

The “rehearsal” framing is reinforced by other research: Gray (2019) found that younger female audiences not only reported self-protective motivation alongside victim empathy, but spent significantly more time on the genre — a finding consistent with heavier investment in building a threat model. Researchers Osiol, DiPietro, and colleagues (2025, Florida Communication Journal) put it more plainly, quoting participants: true crime “gives women a sense of security to know what to watch out for.”

Security. Not entertainment. Security.

The Paradox of Safe Danger#

There’s another mechanism at work that’s worth naming separately: the parasocial container.

Tompkins (2024, published in Villanova’s CONCEPT) describes true crime podcasts as uniquely positioned to foster parasocial relationships with hosts — and through hosts, with the victims and cases they discuss. This matters because parasocial relationships with trusted figures create a psychological holding environment. The fear is real — victim identification activates genuine self-relevant fear — but it’s experienced inside a structure that implies control. There’s a narrator. There’s an arc. The investigation is framed. The case, if not the outcome, has shape.

Noe (2022) describes the podcast format specifically as enabling “safe intimacy with danger.” The intimacy is important — a voice in your ears, often a familiar voice, discussing violence feels closer than watching it. But “safe” is doing real work in that phrase. You are genuinely closer to the threat than you’d be watching a thriller, and simultaneously insulated from it in ways that a real-world threat would not allow. That combination — proximity without consequence — is what makes podcasts distinctively effective as simulative vehicles. You don’t need to manage actual adrenaline. You need to practice the thoughts.

Coduto (2025, Psychology of Popular Media) introduces the term “forensic fandom” to describe communities that treat real crime with the engagement typically reserved for fiction: building timelines, debating suspects, maintaining parasocial relationships with both victims and, more disturbingly, perpetrators. This is the part of the genre that earns some of the criticism it receives. The line between careful engagement and something closer to voyeurism or even perverse fascination is real, and the research doesn’t pretend otherwise.

Not Either/Or: The Dark and the Empathic Both Show Up#

One thing researchers have consistently found is that the true crime audience is not a homogenous type. A 2025 study from Perchtold-Stefan, Rominger, Ceh, and colleagues — published in the British Journal of Psychology (Wiley) and among the more cited recent contributions — found that true crime fascination correlates simultaneously with dark personality traits (hostility, sensation-seeking) and with empathy and conscientiousness. Not one or the other. Both, in different people, with different consumption patterns.

The headline is boring but important: the audience is heterogeneous. There are people who come for the darkness and people who come for the justice. There are people who identify primarily with victims and people who are drawn, with some discomfort, toward the perpetrators. Holmes and Hines (2025, Critical Studies in Television, Sage) found that women in the genre often describe themselves in empathy-and-justice terms while simultaneously acknowledging voyeuristic elements in their own engagement — and that both things are true at once. The self-report and the behavior aren’t contradicting each other. They’re accurately capturing a psychologically complex response.

Phillips (2025, University of Michigan) found that empathy and justice framing dominate self-report among true crime consumers, while sensation-seeking better predicts preference for horror. This suggests the genres draw from different motivational wells — which would explain why true crime and horror have overlapping but distinct audiences, and why the “it’s just morbid entertainment” framing captures some of the horror audience and almost none of the true crime one.

The overconsumption picture is real and worth naming: documented effects include anxiety, some degree of desensitization, and in some cases vicarious trauma. These are genuine costs. But the outcomes on the other end of the ledger include cold case resolutions, increased reporting of missing persons, and — though harder to quantify — the cumulative effect of millions of people spending time learning about threat patterns in their environments.

The One Concern Worth Taking Seriously (And Why It’s Still Overstated)#

The fear that true crime teaches people how to commit crimes is almost irresistible as a cultural narrative and almost entirely unsupported as empirical fact.

LiCausi (2017) found, in an honors thesis at LIU, that some incarcerated individuals acknowledged having consumed crime media and finding elements of it useful. But the study itself acknowledges the obvious causality problem: predisposed people seek out crime media. The content doesn’t produce the disposition; the disposition produces the consumption. McDaniel (2025, Netflix and Kill) applies social learning theory to argue for “acquired capability” development through violent media — a more sophisticated argument, but one that remains contested and poorly supported at the behavioral level.

Meanwhile, the behavior change evidence on the protective side is consistently stronger. White (2025) found true crime consumption predicting heightened vigilance, safety measures, and route planning changes. Scrivner’s simulation framework explicitly predicts that pattern recognition of threat serves defensive rather than offensive purposes. The asymmetry in evidence is significant: we have clear documentation of protective behavioral changes, and very little documentation of offensive ones.

This doesn’t mean the concern is absurd. It means it shouldn’t anchor the conversation.

What Your Brain Is Actually Doing#

Pull it back to the cleanest version of the argument.

You live in a world that is, by nearly every historical measure, extraordinarily safe. The threats that most people in industrialized countries actually face — violence, predation, sudden catastrophe — are probabilistically remote in ways that would have been unimaginable to your ancestors. But you are running the same brain they ran. That brain has a strong, non-negotiable drive to map the threat environment, maintain a current model of what dangers exist and how they work, and rehearse responses to scenarios it hasn’t encountered yet.

That drive, in the ancestral environment, was satisfied by actual experience with actual threat. You saw violence. You learned from it. You updated your model.

In the modern environment, the drive doesn’t go away because the actual threats are rare. It gets redirected at simulations. And in 2024, the highest-fidelity, most emotionally resonant, most regularly updated simulation of real-world human threat patterns is a true crime podcast.

This is not a perfect system. The content is often extracted from real human suffering, the parasocial parasitism gets uncomfortable, and the audience occasionally tips over into something the research can only call forensic fandom with a slightly raised eyebrow. But it is a functional system — functionally analogous to the oral storytelling traditions in every human culture that served exactly this purpose: transmitting knowledge about threat, danger, and survival.

“A good horror story,” Stephen King once wrote, “functions on a symbolic level, using fictional events to help us understand our own deepest real fears.” True crime goes one further: it uses real events, at the cost of the fiction’s insulation, in exchange for a threat model that’s not symbolic at all.

Whether you’re listening because you care about justice, because the case genuinely fascinates you, or because your brain — without your conscious participation — has decided this is how it’s going to practice, you’re not doing something strange. You’re doing something very old.

You’re running a drill.
