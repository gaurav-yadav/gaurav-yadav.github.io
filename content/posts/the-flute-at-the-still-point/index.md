The first thing you hear at Papantla, when the ceremony begins, is not the pole creaking under five men climbing it. It is a small flute, high up, cracked and breathy, and a drum no bigger than a teacup. The drum is strapped to the caporal's left wrist; he strikes it with the same hand. The flute is in his right. He plays both at once. He plays them from the top of a thirty-metre pole, on a wooden platform roughly the size of a dinner plate, with no safety line and nothing to lean against. Around him, four men tie ropes around their waists, tip backward into space, head-first, and begin to spiral slowly downward.

The music is not accompaniment. It is what keeps the ritual from coming apart.

Each flyer will make thirteen revolutions around the pole on the way down. Four flyers times thirteen is fifty-two, which — according to most accounts of the ritual — maps to the Mesoamerican calendar round, the cycle in which the 260-day ritual calendar and the 365-day solar calendar realign. The caporal (the leader, the one who stays at the top) plays a sequence of short ritual tunes called *sones*, each addressed to one of the four cardinal directions. He turns to face each direction in turn. There is a *son de perdón* — a song of forgiveness to the tree whose trunk became the pole. There is a *son de despedida* — a song of farewell as the flyers release. The flute tells the flyers, beat by beat, where they are in a cycle that cannot be allowed to drift.

Remove the music and you do not have a quieter ritual. You have no ritual at all. The sound is the timekeeping, the compass, the choreography, and the blessing. It is also the thing that holds four men, suspended by rope at a lethal height, inside a legible structure of prayer.

## A reed cut from the reed-bed

Four thousand miles and five centuries east of Papantla, in Konya in what is now central Turkey, a thirteenth-century Persian mystic named Jalaluddin Rumi (1207–1273) sat down to compose a long poem. It ran, across the last twelve years of his life, to roughly 25,000 verses. He called it the *Masnavi*. It is among the great religious works of any language, and it opens with the voice of a flute:

> *Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale,*
> *complaining of separations —*
> *Since I was severed from the reed-bed,*
> *men and women have lamented my state.*

— *Masnavi* I:1–3, translated by Jawid Mojaddedi (Oxford, 2004)

The flute Rumi is listening to is the **ney** — an end-blown reed flute, a length of hollow cane with a row of finger-holes cut down its side. No mouthpiece. No reed. No key mechanism. The player holds one end of the tube obliquely against his lips and directs a thin edge of breath across the rim. The sound is produced almost entirely by the angle of the breath.

It is one of the hardest wind instruments in the world to begin on. A beginner can hold a ney for weeks and produce no note at all. Producing a *good* note — the particular breathy, slightly cracked, hovering tone the instrument is prized for — is the work of years. The Turkish term for this characteristic tone is *perdeli*: "veiled." The breath is audible. You hear the player's lungs. This is the opposite of the Western concert-flute aesthetic, where the goal is to hide the mechanics behind a pure, even tone. The ney's aesthetic is to make the cut in the reed and the breath passing through it both audible, at all times. The instrument is showing you its wound.

Which matches the poem. The reed — the actual cane — cannot produce music until it has been severed from the bed where it grew. The cry is the cut. The longing is the music. Rumi's Sufism — the mystical stream of Islam that his poetry articulates — treats separation from the divine as the condition, not the problem, of being a self. The pain of distance is what the path runs on. Settle the pain and you lose the path.

After Rumi's death, his son Sultan Walad formalised his father's teaching into a dervish order — a community of initiates — that came to be known as the Mevlevi. (A dervish is a Sufi initiate in a specific order; the word can loosely mean "the one at the door.") The Mevlevi ceremony is called the **Sema**: the ritual in which dervishes whirl, right hand turned up to receive, left hand turned down to transmit, bodies rotating on a single heel while the skirt rises into a cone. The Sema opens with a solo ney improvisation — *baş taksim*, "the first taking" — performed before anyone moves. The dervishes are seated on their sheepskins. The ney plays alone into the room. When the whirling begins, the ney is the melodic spine of the ensemble — flanked by drums, strings, and voice, but the reed flute at the centre. At the end of the ceremony, the ney plays one last solo and the room is dismissed. The whole thing opens with a reed and closes with a reed.

There is a pun in all of this that the translation cannot carry. *Ney* in Persian means "reed." It is also homophonous with the Persian (and Arabic) *nay*, which is a way of saying "not" or "nothing." The *neyzen* — the master ney player — is simultaneously playing the reed and playing the *nothing*. Rumi's original readers would have heard both meanings at once. In the Sufi apophatic tradition, the path runs through negation first: *la ilaha illa-llah*, "there is no god but God," with the *no* preceding the *but*. The ney sounds the no.

This is not a minor pun. It is one reason the ney was the only instrument Rumi could have placed at the opening of the *Masnavi*. The flute that sounds separation also sounds negation. In Sufi grammar, these are the same move.

## The still point

Here is the observation I did not see coming, running the library through this material.

In the Voladores, a man stands still at the top of a thirty-metre pole and plays a small reed flute while four men spiral in rotation down the pole around him. He does not turn; they turn around him. The flute is at the still centre; the bodies at risk are at the circumference.

In the Sema, one ney player — part of a seated ensemble, but functionally the centre of the sound — plays the reed while the dervishes whirl in rotation around the seated musicians. The ney player does not turn; the dervishes turn around the voice of his flute. Reed flute at the still point; bodies at the circumference.

These two rituals cannot have influenced each other. The Voladores is pre-Columbian, documented in Mesoamerican sources before Spanish arrival, and its lineage traces back through Huastec and Nahua intermediaries to the Aztec *Xocotl Huetzi* festival. The Sema is thirteenth-century Anatolia, formalised by Rumi's son some years after his father's death. There is no plausible transmission path between them. The Totonac caporal and the Mevlevi neyzen are strangers to one another across thousands of years of separate cultural history.

The sonic architecture is the same anyway. A reed flute, sounded from stillness, at the centre of a ritual of turning.

I want to be careful about what this is and is not. It is not a claim that the two traditions share a cosmology. The whirling of the Mevlevi is about the soul's ascent through states of love toward *fana* — the Sufi term for annihilation in the divine. The descent of the Voladores is about the renewal of the Mesoamerican calendar cycle and the fertility of the ground. Different theologies, different gods, different destinations.

What the two rituals share is a *functional constraint*. When a ritual requires human bodies to turn on a vertical axis, and the turning has to be synchronised, and the quality the ritual is producing is an altered attention to time — then a sound-source placed at the still centre, producing a continuous voice the turners can hear, is very nearly a forced design choice. Put it another way: the architecture is convergent because the functional problem is tightly constrained enough that the available solutions are few.

This is a different kind of "same" than the visual pole-and-pillar resemblance the first piece in this series tracked. Tall trees give every culture a predictably shaped ritual object, but the rituals that use them diverge rapidly once you look closely. The sound architecture runs deeper. Function is a tighter constraint than form. When a culture has to solve the specific problem of *how do we synchronise turning bodies around a vertical centre*, the answers available are genuinely few, and cultures that never met arrive at the same one.

## The one-person band

The caporal's particular trick — a small flute played with one hand while the other strikes a small drum, solo, while moving — is unusual enough that when you first meet it you assume it must be unique. It isn't.

Europe has a whole family of traditions built on the same design. In Provence, the **galoubet-tambourin**: a small high pipe held in the left hand, a long narrow drum strapped to the left forearm, struck with a stick in the right hand, played by one person. The Basque Country, on both sides of the French-Spanish border, has the **txistu**, a similar three-hole pipe and tabor combination, played by a civic figure called the *txistulari* who leads public processions, weddings, and the *aurresku*, the honour dance. Catalonia has the *flaviol i tamborí*, the small flute at the heart of the sardana, the Catalan round dance. Medieval England and France had pipe-and-tabor players who appear in the illuminated miniatures of the thirteenth-century *Cantigas de Santa Maria*, a manuscript produced at the court of Alfonso X of Castile. English Morris dancing still uses a pipe-and-tabor leader: one musician walking alongside the dancers, playing flute and drum at once, holding the whole troupe inside a single rhythmic frame.

In every case — Provence, the Basque towns, Catalonia, medieval Latin Europe, English Morris sides, and the Totonac caporal — you have the same design: a three-hole flute played one-handed, a small drum played with the other hand, one body carrying both.

The three-hole constraint is the clever part. With only three fingerholes you would think you could produce only a handful of notes. In fact a skilled player, by varying breath pressure and using overblowing to reach higher harmonics, can sound a full diatonic octave and a half. The instrument was **designed around** the constraint: build a flute a person could play with one hand, so the same body can carry the beat. The design is counter-intuitive until you notice what it enables.

Within Europe, the regional forms are transmission-linked. By the thirteenth century, pipe-and-tabor was a pan-European phenomenon — attested from England to Iberia — and the modern survivals in the Basque towns, Catalonia, Provence, and English Morris are continuous descendants of that medieval Latin-European tradition. There are reasonable questions about whether the European tradition in turn came in from an earlier Byzantine or Islamic-world source. The *Cantigas* attestation at Alfonso's court, which sat at the Islamic-Christian Iberian frontier, is suggestive. But within Europe, at least, the lineage is more or less one tree.

The Mesoamerican case is the one that will not fit onto that tree. The Voladores ritual pre-dates Spanish contact; the Totonac flute is a local cane or reed, not the galoubet; the drum is a small wrist-sized hand drum, not the long narrow tambourin; the musical form is the *son*, not the European pipe repertoire. No transmission channel runs between medieval European pipe-and-tabor and pre-Columbian Totonac ritual. They cannot share an ancestor.

So what explains the agreement? The same thing that explains the ney and the caporal. The functional problem is the same. A single ritual specialist has to provide both beat and melody to dancers who are moving around him — and he himself has to be able to move. He has to walk, turn, climb, or stand above the dancers on a platform. He cannot sit behind a larger ensemble. He needs one body's worth of sound, covering both rhythm and pitch, with both hands free to hold the instruments and his feet free to move the rest of him. The physical constraints admit very few answers. A three-hole pipe, a drum strapped to the wrist or forearm, one person. This is the solution the problem allows. Different cultures find it when they come up against the problem.

This is one of the cleaner cases I know for thinking about how functional pressure shapes ritual form. We do not need ancient inheritance or a mysterious cultural substrate. We need only to notice that the ritual specialist needed both hands free while moving, and that there is a narrow family of sound-objects that work under those conditions. Everything else is regional colour — the specific reed, the specific skin of the drum, the specific tuning of the pipe.

## The drum that carries

Now shift. The pattern I have been tracking — a still sound-source at the centre of a ritual of turning bodies — is not the only way cultures use sound on a vertical axis. There is another pattern, shaped by a completely different functional problem.

A shaman — a ritual specialist in societies distributed across Siberia, Mongolia, the Sami lands of Lapland, and (with connections that are plausible but not provable) parts of the Americas — sits alone. There are no dancers. There is no ensemble. There is one practitioner and one instrument, and the instrument is a **frame drum**: a single-headed membrane stretched over a circular wooden hoop, held in the left hand, struck with a beater in the right.

The shaman plays for minutes to hours at a steady pulse — roughly four to eight beats per second. Sustained. Unchanging. In modern neuroscience this pulse falls in what is called the **theta range**: brain-rhythm frequencies associated with drowsiness, deep meditation, and certain altered states of awareness. A literature going back to Michael Harner's *The Way of the Shaman* (1980) has argued that sustained theta-frequency drumming entrains the listener's brainwaves and induces the altered consciousness the shaman uses to journey. Laboratory work partially supports the entrainment claim, though the evidence is less clean than core-shamanism advocates tend to suggest. Hold that hedge.

What the practitioners themselves say about the drum matters more than the neurophysiology. Across remarkably diverse traditions — Siberian Evenk, Tuvan, Yakut, Sami, Mongolian, Altaic — the drum is described through a consistent cluster of metaphors. The drum is the shaman's **horse** (most widely, across Siberia and Mongolia). The drum is the shaman's **boat** (among peoples whose cosmology places the upper world across water). In some Altaic traditions, the drum is the shaman's **bow** and the beater is the arrow. Among the Samoyed, the drum *is* the world tree. These are not decorative metaphors. They are operational descriptions. The shaman is going somewhere, and the drum is how he gets there.

The somewhere has a direction. The shaman is not going inward, or deeper into himself, or into the unconscious. Those are modern framings that would have puzzled the people who built the practice. The shaman goes **up** — to the sky realms, to the upper spirits, to the clan ancestors — or **down**, to the lower world, to the spirits of illness, to the dead. The horizontal social world is left behind. The vertical cosmic world is entered. The drum moves him along that vertical.

Many Siberian shaman drums are inscribed, on the inner or outer face, with a **cosmological map**: three vertical zones for upper, middle, and lower worlds, often with a central tree or pole running up the middle, and spirits, animals, and ancestors placed in their proper zones. The drum is a picture of the cosmos the shaman is about to travel through. And the drum is physically made from that cosmos. The wood of the frame comes from a specific tree chosen by spirit revelation, sometimes a tree that has been struck by lightning — one already touched by the upper world. The circuit is closed. The cosmos is drawn on the drum. The drum is made from the cosmos. The drum sounds. The shaman enters the cosmos. The instrument is not a representation. It is the vehicle, the map, and the origin all in one object.

Notice what this architecture is not doing. It is not synchronising turning bodies. There are no turning bodies. It is not producing a protective rhythmic envelope for dancers at risk. There are no dancers. The solo practitioner is inside the ritual architecture and the sound is what moves him through it. The functional problem is different from the Voladores-or-Sema case, and the sonic solution is correspondingly different. A frame drum, played alone, can do this work. A reed flute, played alone, cannot.

The frame drum's shared features across the circumpolar belt — single-headed, round, hand-held, wooden hoop, owned by one specific practitioner, cosmologically inscribed, used to induce journey rather than possession, horse-or-boat metaphor — are consistent and specific enough that scholars tend to read the circumpolar tradition as a genuinely shared ancient technology, plausibly carried across Beringia with the settlement of the Americas and preserved in regional variation ever since. Unlike the pipe-and-tabor case, where the European forms are linked by medieval transmission and the Mesoamerican case stands alone, the frame drum looks like one long, continuous cousinship — a Palaeolithic inheritance with different descendants in different regions. The best corrective to the over-unifying thesis in Eliade's 1951 *Shamanism* is not to reject the shared substrate but to refuse to reduce every later ritual to it. The substrate is real. Its descendants are local.

## What sound does on the vertical

Three patterns, three architectures, three different answers to the question: *what is music doing, in a ritual built on the vertical?*

The caporal's pipe-and-tabor and its European cousins sound music as the rhythmic envelope that holds bodies moving at risk around a still centre. The ritual specialist is that centre; the dancers or flyers are the circumference; the sound holds the arrangement inside a single synchronised frame.

The ney of the Mevlevi Sema sounds music as the audible voice of separation — the cry of the cut by which a soul is made a self in the first place — and so, in Sufi reading, as the sound of the longing that is the path home. The whirling is the body's answer to the reed.

The frame drum of the circumpolar shaman sounds music as a vehicle. The sound is not around the practitioner and not addressed to an audience. It carries him along an axis his body has no other way of reaching. The drum is the horse he rides.

These are not the same use of sound. The pipe-and-tabor is a synchronisation device. The ney is a theological utterance. The frame drum is a vehicle. Running them together as "ritual music" — the way anthropology textbooks sometimes do — obscures what each one is actually for.

What they share is an underlying recognition, and it is the recognition, not the architecture, that matters. In every case, the sound is what makes the vertical passable. The body on its own cannot climb the sky. A flute can tell turning bodies where they are in a calendar cycle they are physically enacting. A reed can articulate the cut by which a soul knows itself exiled from its source, and so can become the companion of its longing home. A drum can carry consciousness along a seam the body has no way to follow. Three functional problems. Three sonic solutions. One underlying intuition: some places cannot be reached any other way.

Sound goes where bodies can't.

## The thing empires did not notice

I want to close on a thread I do not intend to resolve here.

The first piece in this series noted in passing that several of the body-risking rituals in its catalogue were banned in a single nineteenth-century wave. The hook-swinging version of Charak Puja, the Bengali devotional rite in which devotees are suspended from a pole by iron hooks through the skin of the back, was outlawed by the British colonial administration by Act XIV of 1865. The Sun Dance, the central Plains ceremony involving dancers tethered to a pole by skewers through the chest, was outlawed by the United States in 1883 and by Canada in 1895. Empires can see a man hanging from hooks. They can read what they see, and decide to make it stop.

In the same decades, in the same subcontinent, under the same colonial administration, a different Sufi devotional practice was flourishing largely undisturbed. **Qawwali**: the ecstatic devotional song of the South Asian Sufi shrines — performed in the courtyards of the *dargahs*, the tombs of Sufi saints, by hereditary ensembles called *qawwals*. The form is designed to induce *hāl*, a state of spiritual arousal that tips at its deepest into ecstatic trance, in the listener. It uses Rumi's verses along with those of later Sufi poets — notably Amir Khusro in fourteenth-century Delhi, who is regarded as the tradition's formaliser — and pushes them into the body through rhythmic repetition and melodic intensification. It was, and still is, a body-involving devotional practice doing much of the same theological work that the Charak hook-swinging was doing: carrying the devotee across a boundary of ordinary selfhood into a state in which the divine becomes present.

It was not banned. It was not specifically suppressed. Individual shrines came under occasional colonial scrutiny, but as a form, Qawwali moved through the nineteenth century mostly unmolested.

Why? Part of the answer is that the British administrative taxonomy classified Qawwali as *music* rather than *ritual*. Music was not an administrative priority. The reform-minded colonial officer had been trained to see a man with hooks through his back as a ritual, and therefore as an object of humanitarian concern. He had been trained to see a group of men singing in a shrine courtyard as music, and therefore as a cultural object of low priority. The categorisation was doing load-bearing work. What got banned and what did not hung, to a disconcerting extent, on which administrative box the thing fell into.

There is more to say here, and the third piece in this series is where I intend to say it. For now, set this observation down: across the rituals we have been looking at — the turning bodies at Papantla, the whirling dervishes in Konya, the shaman alone in his tent, the hook-swinger on the Bengal pole — a pattern keeps offering itself. Sound survives where ritual does not. Music travels where body-practice cannot. Some of this is because sound is portable and ritual is place-bound. Some of it is because sound can be recorded and ritual can only be witnessed. But some of it, I suspect, is because the empires that did the classifying did not always see what the music was doing.

Music goes where bodies can't. Sometimes because the bodies are not welcome. Sometimes because the classifier isn't watching.
