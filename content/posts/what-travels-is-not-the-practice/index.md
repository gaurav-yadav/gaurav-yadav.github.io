In 1865, the Council of the Governor-General of India, meeting at Calcutta, passed a short law. Act XIV of that year forbade any person from suspending another, or being suspended, from a pole by iron hooks driven through the skin of the back. The law was directed at a single ritual. The hook-swinging component of **Charak Puja** — a devotional rite held at the end of the Bengali year, dedicated to Shiva, in which devotees drove iron hooks into the muscles of their own backs and were then swung in the air from the top of a tall bamboo pole — had been a publicly visible part of Bengali religious life for at least several centuries. The men who swung were swinging as prayer. Act XIV named the practice cruelty and made it a crime.

The Act did not come out of nowhere. It was the outcome of decades of metropolitan lobbying — by British Baptist missionaries headquartered at Serampore, by a Bengali middle-class reformist stratum that had aligned itself with the missionary position, by anthropologists whose ethnographies had been laundered through parliamentary debate. Neither did the Act succeed in the clean way its sponsors intended. Hook-swinging did not stop. It went sideways and waited. Rope-suspension forms replaced iron hooks in many towns. The rite thinned, persisted, shifted venue. By the twentieth century the hooks had quietly returned in several districts. The ban technically stands to this day and is functionally unenforced.

This is, more or less, the story most readers will have met before: a colonial administration bans a body-risking indigenous devotional practice on humanitarian grounds; the practice survives anyway. The story is true. It is also, for our purposes here, not the interesting story.

The interesting story is that Act XIV of 1865 was not one event. It was one of four, spread across three decades, passed by three different imperial legislatures on two continents, each targeting a different ritual, all four sharing the same rhetorical template, the same list of disapproved practices, and the same metropolitan humanitarian framing. Something did travel the networks of nineteenth-century empire. It was not hook-swinging. It was not the Sun Dance. It was not any of the banned rites.

What traveled was the template for banning them.

## A connected event, not a coincidence

Here is the sequence, laid out.

**1865, Calcutta.** Act XIV of the Bengal Council. Hook-swinging in Charak Puja is outlawed under British India.

**1883, Washington.** The Rules for Indian Courts of Indian Offenses — generally known as the Religious Crimes Code — issued by the United States Commissioner of Indian Affairs. Its list of prohibited practices includes "the sun-dance, and all other similar dances and so-called religious ceremonies." The Sun Dance is the Plains nations' central renewal ceremony; its piercing form had already become a specific target for missionary complaint.

**1894, Madras.** The Madras Prohibition of Hook-Swinging Act, extending the Bengal Act XIV logic down the south-Indian coast. Essentially the same law, in substantially similar language, with the same humanitarian rationale, passed by a different presidency of the same colonial state some twenty-nine years later.

**1895, Ottawa.** An amendment to the Canadian Indian Act — the foundational legislation governing Canadian policy toward First Nations — strengthening an earlier 1884 Potlatch prohibition and expanding it to cover the Sun Dance and other ceremonies. The Potlatch is a ceremonial gift-exchange central to many Pacific Northwest nations; its banning had been the 1884 opening move, to which the 1895 amendment added further prohibitions.

Four laws. Three decades. Three imperial capitals. One rhetorical shape.

Each law targeted a ritual that fell into the same narrow cluster of characteristics. Body-risking. Ecstatic. Communal. Performed in public. Indigenous. Not yet assimilated to Christian reformist respectability. The rituals themselves were unrelated in any cosmological sense — Charak Puja is a Shaiva rite, the Sun Dance a Plains-nations renewal ceremony, the Potlatch a Northwest Coast gift-economy ceremony — but every one of them met that specific bundle of criteria. And every one of them was singled out for prohibition inside the same thirty-year window.

The odds that four such laws, passed by independent legislative bodies in three imperial capitals, targeting distinct unrelated rituals, within thirty years of each other, are independent events are vanishingly low. They are one event, in motion. What we have to explain is how an event can travel that way: along which networks, carried by which named people, on the authority of which prior document.

## The named carriers

The rhetorical template these bans ran on is older than any one of them. It was written in London in 1830.

**James Peggs** (c. 1794–1850) was an English Baptist missionary who had served in Orissa during the 1820s and returned to Britain to lobby for the reform of Indian practices he had witnessed there. In 1830 he published a book called *India's Cries to British Humanity*. Its chapters catalogued practices Peggs and his missionary network believed the British Parliament had a moral duty to ban: sati (widow-burning), infant exposure, the drowning of pilgrims at Puri, and hook-swinging. The rhetorical method was consistent across the chapters. *Describe the practice. Quote a witness. Call it cruelty. Invoke Britain's new duty, after the abolition of the slave trade in 1807 and slavery in 1833, to extend humanitarian intervention to its imperial subjects. Demand legislation.*

The method is what got reused. The specific rites listed in the book changed, the parliamentary pressure moved through different cycles, the legislation took decades to produce in each case. But the portable form — *atrocity-documentation aimed at Parliament* — was a template. Peggs was not the template's inventor; the same move had been used in the anti-sati campaign that produced Regulation XVII of 1829. He was its compiler and amplifier in the register that worked for Indian ritual.

Peggs was not working alone. He was part of a broader coalition of Baptist missionaries based at Serampore, the Danish enclave on the Hooghly River upstream from Calcutta where **William Carey** (1761–1834) had established the first modern Protestant mission station in India in 1800. Carey — translator of the Bible into Bengali, Sanskrit, and several other languages, founding figure of subcontinental Protestant mission work — was dead long before Act XIV passed in 1865. But the Serampore network he built was the channel through which missionary observation of Indian ritual reached metropolitan Britain for two generations. The Baptist Missionary Society and the Church Missionary Society, working through Serampore and through their own stations, provided the continuous flow of reports and petitions that kept hook-swinging visible as a parliamentary issue through decades in which no one in Westminster had any other reason to notice it.

By the 1840s and 1850s a Bengali middle-class reformist stratum — the social descendants of Rammohan Roy and his circle — had aligned itself with the missionary position on hook-swinging. The historian G.A. Oddie's 1995 study *Popular Religion, Elites and Reforms* documents the Bengali side of the coalition: the regulation became politically workable once it could claim a "native" constituency to pair with the metropolitan one. The missionary framing had taken hold among the administrative class of the colonised population. Act XIV of 1865 passed because the people whose voices colonial governance listened to had both converged on calling hook-swinging cruelty.

The template then crossed the Atlantic.

**Hiram Price** (1814–1901) was Commissioner of Indian Affairs in the United States from 1881 to 1885, under President Chester A. Arthur. He was an Iowa Methodist and a former congressman, brought in as part of a reform-minded "peace policy" push that believed federal policy could civilise the Plains nations through Christian-style administration. In April 1883 Price issued the *Rules for Indian Courts of Indian Offenses*. The document is short — a page and a half of listed offenses, with attached procedures. It created tribunals of Indian judges (appointed by the federal government, not elected by their communities) at each reservation, and set out what those tribunals were to punish. One paragraph of the list banned the Sun Dance, alongside give-away feasts, plural marriage, and the influence of "medicine men." Henry M. Teller, Secretary of the Interior under Arthur, signed off.

Price had been reading the kind of missionary ethnography Peggs had been writing. The rhetorical template in the Code's preamble — body-risk identified as cruelty, ceremony framed as civilizational impediment, humanitarian duty invoked, metropolitan moral authority assumed — is recognisably the same shape. Different continent, different ritual, same form of argument. Price did not cite Peggs. He did not have to. The template had by then become a feature of the evangelical reform air.

In Canada the arc is the same, the names different. The 1884 amendment to the Indian Act banning the Potlatch was the opening move; the 1895 amendment, which added the Sun Dance and other ceremonies, the strengthening. **Duncan Campbell Scott** (1862–1947) was not the author of the 1895 amendment — he was still young at the time — but he rose through the Department of Indian Affairs to become its Deputy Superintendent from 1913 to 1932 and was, for two decades, the chief administrative architect of the broader assimilation policy that the ceremonial bans were part of. He ran the residential school system. He wrote the enforcement policy. He is the most frequently named Canadian administrator in the suppression's late phase, and he is also — and this is the part that is hard to hold steady — a published poet of some critical reputation in his own lifetime. His verse includes the often-anthologised "The Onondaga Madonna," a sonnet in which the poet mourns the passing of a civilisation his bureaucratic day job was, at the time of writing, actively dismantling. The figure is not a curiosity. He is what the category-carrier looks like when the category and the person have fused. A literary sensibility that ran toward elegy for the cultures his administrative sensibility was helping to end.

None of these men met each other. Peggs was dead before Price was appointed. Carey was dead before Peggs published. Scott was a schoolboy when Price issued the Code. They did not need to meet. They read each other's lineage — the same reform periodicals, the same Evangelical and Methodist quarterlies, the same missionary society annual reports, the same anthropological journals that had begun, by the 1870s and 1880s, to classify body-ordeal as "primitive survival." James Mill's 1817 *History of British India* had framed Indian civilisation as arrested. James Frazer's later comparative work would supply a scientific-sounding vocabulary for calling any ecstatic bodily ordeal a "survival" that a modern state should help its subjects to outgrow. The cited authors shifted across the century. The underlying template did not.

This is what a transmission looks like when what is being transmitted is a category. There is no shipment. No messenger. What moves is the way a thing is thought — the administrative filter through which the observed ritual is passed before the legislator acts on it.

## Why Qawwali survived

This is where the story turned, for me, running it.

In the same subcontinent as Charak Puja, under the same colonial administration, during the same decades that saw hook-swinging outlawed twice, a different devotional practice that did much of the same work was doing fine.

*Qawwali* is the ecstatic Sufi devotional song tradition of South Asia. *Sufi* is the mystical stream of Islam; Qawwali developed within the Chishti Sufi order in thirteenth- and fourteenth-century Delhi and spread with Chishti dargahs across the subcontinent. A *dargah* is the tomb of a Sufi saint; it functions, in the practice that grew up around it, as a living shrine where the saint's intercession is sought. Qawwali is performed in the courtyard of such a shrine by a hereditary ensemble called a *qawwal*. The music is organised around texts — Persian ghazals by Rumi, Urdu devotional poems, verses by the subcontinent's own Sufi poets — and it is designed to induce in the listener a state called *hāl*: spiritual arousal, sometimes tipping into ecstatic trance. A Qawwali gathering (*mahfil-e-sama*, literally a "gathering for listening") is not a concert. The performance adjusts to the state of the room. Verses get extended or repeated when a listener enters hāl. The music presses until something happens. At its deepest, a Qawwali listener can pass out, weep uncontrollably, or appear temporarily not to know where they are.

This has been well documented for centuries. It was documented during the nineteenth century too. The relevant colonial officer could have encountered it in any of several dozen dargah courtyards in Delhi, Ajmer, Lahore, or Hyderabad. He could have read about it in any of the orientalist surveys that had been mapping Indian religion since the early 1800s. It was not hidden.

It is also — fairly obviously, on reflection — body-involving. The listener's body is the medium of the music's effect. The state the music is aiming for is an altered condition of consciousness indistinguishable, in most observable ways, from the hook-swinger's trance or the Sun Dancer's vision. Qawwali and the banned rites were doing, in neighbouring devotional dialects, the same kind of work.

And yet the British colonial administration did not ban Qawwali. It did not regulate Qawwali. By and large it barely noticed Qawwali. Individual Sufi shrines occasionally came under administrative scrutiny — usually on anti-sedition or fakir-vagrancy grounds, not on ritual ones — but the form itself moved through the suppression era unmolested. Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan, the great twentieth-century qawwal whose recordings reached a global audience in the 1980s, was born in 1948 into a family lineage whose training had continued uninterrupted across the entire colonial period. The nineteenth century was, for Qawwali by the standards of the other rites in this essay, an era of relative stability.

What accounts for the difference? This is the piece's sharpest claim, so let me frame it as an argument rather than a finding.

The British administrative apparatus had inherited, from the metropolitan reform movement, a specific set of categories for thinking about indigenous practice. *Ritual* was one category, and it was a high-priority target for reform. *Body-ordeal* was a subcategory inside ritual, and it was the highest priority within the highest priority. *Music*, on the other hand, was a different category entirely, and it was a much lower-priority one. Music was something colonial officers might collect, transcribe, sponsor, patronise, or (with appropriate orientalist condescension) enjoy. It was not, typically, a thing they felt any pressure to ban.

When a colonial officer attended a Qawwali gathering — or, more usually, read a report by someone who had attended one — what he saw was filtered through the category "music." The listeners' physical states, the swaying, the tears, the crying out, the verses being repeated until the room's emotional pitch shifted — these registered, in his administrative vocabulary, as musical intensity, as aesthetic experience. They did not register as ritual ordeal. Nobody was suspended from a pole by iron hooks. Nobody's chest was being pierced. Qawwali was filed under "music." Music was not a target. Qawwali was left alone.

The classifier's categories were load-bearing. What got banned and what did not hung, to a disconcerting extent, on which administrative box the thing fell into. A devotional practice that induced altered consciousness through sustained rhythmic singing was a musical form. A devotional practice that induced altered consciousness through visible body-risk was a ritual cruelty. The underlying phenomenon — an ecstatic state induced by disciplined religious means as a path toward the divine — produced opposite bureaucratic responses because it fell into different metropolitan boxes.

A necessary hedge. I am not arguing that Qawwali was untouched by the colonial encounter. It was. The harmonium entered the South Asian musical palette under colonial commerce and has been a Qawwali instrument ever since. The gramophone arrived in the 1900s and reshaped what a performance could be. The shrine economy was restructured by Bengal-Bombay-Delhi urbanisation under British rule. And some individual dargahs did face sustained administrative pressure, usually in ways that had nothing to do with the music and everything to do with anti-sedition worry about the gatherings' political uses. But the *form* of Qawwali was not prohibited, and the devotional work it does continued through the suppression century without the dislocations the hook-swingers and the Sun Dancers had to absorb.

This is not a story about Qawwali being specifically protected. It is a story about a specific administrative taxonomy — the inherited metropolitan distinction between "ritual" (to be reformed) and "music" (to be collected) — making some things targets and leaving others alone. The filter was what traveled. Once you see it, you start to notice its fingerprints in other corners of the nineteenth-century reform record as well.

## The one time a ritual actually did travel

A moment for the counter-example. It matters for the argument, and it is worth stopping on.

Across the full axis-mundi complex that the first two pieces of this series surveyed — a cluster of pole-climbing, body-suspension, and vertical-ascent rituals scattered across five continents and several millennia — there is exactly one fully-documented case of a ritual crossing a cultural boundary, intact, via a specific political channel, on a specific date. It happened in 204 BCE, in Rome, during the Second Punic War.

Hannibal was still in Italy. The war with Carthage had been going badly for more than a decade. The Roman Senate, in its old habit of consulting the **Sibylline books** — a collection of oracular verses kept in the Temple of Jupiter on the Capitoline Hill, consulted by the state in times of crisis — received, by way of the priests who read them, the instruction that if the *Magna Mater* (the "Great Mother," Cybele, a Phrygian goddess whose cult centre was at Pessinus in Anatolia) could be brought to Rome, the invader would leave Italy. The historian Livy describes the episode at *Ab Urbe Condita* 29.10–14. A senatorial delegation was dispatched to the court of **Attalus I of Pergamon**, the Hellenistic king of a wealthy state on the western Anatolian coast. Attalus granted the Romans the goddess's cult object — a black stone, almost certainly a meteorite, of the kind Anatolian religion had long treated as an aniconic emanation of divinity. The stone was carried by ship from Asia Minor to the Tiber. The cult was installed on the Palatine Hill. The cult personnel came with it, including the *galli*: the ecstatic eunuch priests of Cybele, ritually castrated at their initiation.

For the next five centuries the Roman state sat in an ambivalent relation to what it had imported. Roman citizens were legally forbidden from joining the galli — self-castration remained, in the Roman political vocabulary, incompatible with citizenship — but the cult itself was licensed, state-supported, and woven into the Roman religious calendar. Every March, the *dendrophori* ("tree-bearers") of Cybele carried a sacred pine through the streets of Rome to the temple on the Palatine, an image of Attis (Cybele's dying consort) bound to its trunk. The ritual had been transplanted whole from Phrygia: tree-bearing, eunuch priesthood, ecstatic procession, cult stone. It kept most of its original structure for half a millennium in its new home.

This is what a genuine ritual transmission looks like. A political decision, documented. A specific embassy to a named king. A physical object transported. A cult personnel relocated. An institutional absorption stretching across centuries. Livy's account names the people, the motivation, the outcome.

The rarity of this case is the point. Inside the entire axis-mundi complex — the Voladores, the Sun Dance, Onbashira at Suwa, Charak Puja, Naghol on Pentecost Island, the Siberian shaman's birch, the European maypole, the Mesoamerican Xocotl Huetzi, and their kin — Cybele to Rome in 204 BCE is essentially the only clear case of cross-cultural ritual transmission. Everything else is convergent: the vertical pole, the body suspended, the turning ritual specialist, the calendar enacted in rope — these arrive independently in culture after culture because the cosmology the human body assembles under the sky is similar everywhere, not because there is a secret channel moving the theology between continents.

When a ritual does in fact travel, we know. We have the embassy dispatch. We have the senatorial record. We have the Livy passage. We have the physical stone. The paperwork exists. The fact that the entire axis-mundi complex offers essentially one such paper trail tells you what a rare thing the genuine transmission of ritual is. Convergence is the default. Crossing is the exception that proves the rule.

## A short note on Eliade

Before the piece's last move, one callback worth making.

The first piece in this series noted that Mircea Eliade — a twentieth-century Romanian historian of religion, writing in French — had built the comparative category of *axis mundi* in his 1951 book *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*. Eliade assembled rituals from across continents under a single interpretive rubric and named the underlying pattern the rituals were, on his reading, variants of. His category was partly real — emically present, in their own theological vocabulary, in several of the traditions it described — and partly imposed, as the historian Ronald Hutton's 1996 work on the European maypole demonstrated. Eliade's book travelled to every continent where there was a university library. Most of the rituals he catalogued never travelled past their own region.

This is a translation moment of a specific kind. The twentieth-century scholar, working from inside a European comparative-religion tradition, built an interpretive category that became the global lens through which his readers came to understand what ritual across cultures was doing. The lens itself then shaped who got seen, who got banned, and who got later protected. The category is part of the transmission story. Not because Eliade was working with the nineteenth-century colonial bureau — he wasn't — but because the comparative category *itself* is what now moves along the networks the colonial bureau used to move along. The book reaches every anthropology department; the ritual stays in its village.

## The inverse symmetry

The Voladores de Papantla was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2009. UNESCO — the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization — was founded in 1945. The Intangible Cultural Heritage Convention, the legal instrument under which the Voladores is now protected, was adopted in 2003. The first Representative List inscriptions were issued in 2008. The Voladores was in the second cohort.

What UNESCO inscription does, at the level of the apparatus, is close to the inverse of what Act XIV of 1865 did. Both are administrative documents issued by a metropolitan institution, concerning an indigenous ritual performed on distant ground, in a language the classifier does not speak. Both rely on evidence prepared by researchers who have been in the field. Both are the outcome of coordinated lobbying by networked advocacy organisations. Both, once enacted, carry the force of state or interstate authority. Both are, at the level they actually operate at, **category assignments**. In 1865, the ritual was assigned to the category "cruelty, to be extirpated." In 2009, the ritual was assigned to the category "heritage, to be safeguarded."

The rituals are of course different: Charak Puja's hook-swinging in Bengal, the Voladores' pole-flying in Veracruz. But the administrative machineries are structurally similar. And — this is the striking part — the routes along which those machineries operate are, in a recognisable sense, the same routes. The Church Missionary Society, the Baptist Missionary Society, the Bureau of Indian Affairs, the Canadian Department of Indian Affairs ran through networks of metropolitan funding, institutional coordination, transnational information flow, and state-backed authority. UNESCO's Intangible Heritage program runs through networks of metropolitan funding (Paris), institutional coordination (UN agencies), transnational information flow, and state-backed authority (member states). The capital cities have shifted. The political colour has reversed. The infrastructure is recognisably continuous.

The inscription of the Voladores in 2009 came one hundred and forty-four years after the Bengal Act of 1865. Somewhere in those fourteen decades the metropolitan machinery reversed polarity. The same kind of international, institution-based, category-assigning apparatus that once decided a body-risking ritual was cruelty now decides a body-risking ritual is heritage. The decision still belongs to the apparatus, not to the ritual's home community. The Totonac flyers of Papantla are grateful for the UNESCO listing; they also, as several Totonac community members have noted in conversations with researchers, would prefer not to need it. Heritage designation is a mixed inheritance. It can commodify a living practice into a tourist-facing performance. It can freeze into a museum form a ritual that had survived for centuries by being able to change. It can, and often does, reinforce rather than dissolve the asymmetry by which a distant institution decides what a local practice is worth.

I don't want to draw a false equivalence between suppression and protection. Being inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List is, on any honest accounting, better than being prohibited by Act XIV. The point is narrower. The institutional form is inherited. The categories are set by people who are usually strangers to the rituals they are setting categories for. What has changed across the hundred and forty-four years is what the categories do. What has not changed is who gets to decide them.

## What the series has been saying

Put plainly: rituals do not travel well. They survive in place — in the villages of Papantla, in the dargahs of Delhi and Ajmer, on the Plains reservations, on Pentecost Island, at the Suwa shrine in Nagano, in the tekkes of Konya, at the cottonwood tree and at the bamboo pole. The first piece in this series made the case through the visual resemblance of pole-and-pillar rites: most of these rituals are not secretly related, they are independent local solutions to a universal cosmological problem. The second piece made the case through the architecture of ritual sound: similar functional constraints produce similar sonic solutions across cultures that never met, without any transmission channel to account for the resemblance. This piece has tried to supply the missing structural inverse. If rituals themselves do not travel, something else has to account for why some of them survived the nineteenth century and some did not, and why some are now globally recognised and some are still marginal.

The answer this piece has argued for is that the *category* under which the ritual is classified is the thing that travels; the category determines what the administrative apparatus does with the ritual; and the administrative apparatus, more than the ritual itself, is what determines whether a given body-risking devotional practice can legally and visibly happen on its own ground. In the nineteenth century the transmitted category was a reform category: body-ordeal as cruelty, ritual as civilisational impediment. In the twenty-first it is a protection category: intangible heritage as a good to be safeguarded. Different contents. Same infrastructure. The missionary society, the colonial bureau, the heritage agency — these are successive operators of a single category-transmission infrastructure, continuous since the early nineteenth century, that passes categories from the metropolitan centre to the peripheral site where the ritual is actually practised. The infrastructure is what persists. The asymmetry it encodes is what persists with it.

You can stop here if you want. The factual argument is done.

## The uncomfortable part

What I cannot shake, running the library through this material, is that the story has the same shape in either century.

Black Elk, the Oglala Lakota holy man, could explain what the Sun Dance was for, in his own language, on his own ground, in theological terms the Lakota had used for generations. James Peggs could explain in Baptist Bengal-English why the hook-swinging that belonged to other people's tradition should be criminalised. Hiram Price signed the Sun Dance ban in Washington on the authority of reports prepared by Indian agents who had been trained to look at ceremony through Peggs's lens. Mircea Eliade, seventy years after Price, could explain in French what the *axis mundi* meant. UNESCO, half a century after Eliade, inscribed the Voladores on a protected list compiled in Paris. Black Elk's kind of knowledge was locally held and slow to move. The classifier's kind of knowledge was metropolitan and travelled fast. Of the four men named in that sequence after Black Elk, three never set foot on the territories where the rituals they were deciding on had been practised for centuries. The ritual's own language and the community's own voice reached those four through whatever administrative or scholarly filter was currently in fashion in the capital.

The Sun Dance survived. Not because its suppression was defeated in Washington, and not because it was rehabilitated in Paris. It survived because the Lakota and the Cheyenne and the Blackfoot kept dancing it in defiance of a legal regime whose reach grew weaker the further it travelled from its own capital. That is the good news in the argument, and the good news is real. But the kind of survival the argument names is hard-won. The ritual survives by outlasting the category, not by escaping it.

What this tells you, as a reader, about ritual is one thing. What it tells you about categories — the ones we build now, in the current century, and the institutions that carry them — is your own work from here.
