At Papantla, in the Mexican state of Veracruz, five men walk to the base of a thirty-metre wooden pole and begin to climb. Four of them wear red tunics stitched with small mirrors; the fifth carries a small flute and a small drum. When they reach the top — a square platform the size of a dinner table, no guardrail, no net — the fifth stands upright and begins to play. He is the *caporal*: the leader, the one who stays. The other four tie ropes around their waists, climb onto the platform behind him, and tip backward into space, head-first, arms outstretched. A wooden frame at the apex of the pole begins to rotate. The ropes unwind. The four flyers spiral slowly downward in widening circles until their fingers graze the grass at the base.

That's the picture. The arithmetic is stranger than the picture.

Each flyer makes thirteen revolutions around the pole on the way down. Four flyers, thirteen revolutions each, is fifty-two. Most accounts of the ritual say that isn't a coincidence: fifty-two is the length of the Mesoamerican *calendar round*, the cycle in which the 260-day ritual calendar and the 365-day solar calendar realign, the unit by which the Aztec and their neighbours measured the span of a generation. The ritual, on this reading, is a cosmology enacted in rope and wood. The flyers are not performing a calendar; they *are* one, being counted out.

(A single honest hedge up front. The fifty-two-count is consistent across most secondary English-language accounts, but the chain back to a Totonac primary source is thinner than I'd like. Tradition holds. I'm not going to treat it as settled.)

---

We went looking for the Voladores' cousins. Caravanserai, the project that produced this piece, is a knowledge library — a slowly growing collection of cross-referenced notes on how ideas survive transit through time, language, and culture. It has three scouts. One looks after wisdom traditions: Sufi poets, Stoic philosophers, mystical concepts. One keeps track of music and oral story. One catalogues the Silk Road and the long history of what was actually carried between cultures, as opposed to what people later imagined had been. A reader asked a small question: *what other rituals look like this one?*

We sent the scouts. They came back with seven candidates, a cautionary tale, and an argument about reading ritual across cultures that I didn't expect to be making.

## Seven poles

The Voladores look, at first glance, like they have relatives everywhere.

**Charak Puja**, in Bengal and parts of eastern India, closes the Bengali year with a month of austerities culminating in a hook-swinging rite: devotees of Shiva drive iron hooks through the skin of the back and are suspended from a tall bamboo pole — the *charak tree* — swinging in the air. The hook version was banned by the British colonial administration by Act XIV of 1865. It survived the ban. Today many regional forms substitute rope tied around the waist for the hooks, though the hooks have not entirely gone away.

**The Sun Dance**, at the centre of ceremonial life for several Plains Indigenous nations — Lakota, Cheyenne, Blackfoot, Arapaho, and others — organises four days of dancing around a central cottonwood pole that the community cuts, speaks to, and erects with specific protocol. In some forms, dancers are pierced through the chest, tethered to the pole by leather cords, and dance until the flesh tears free. The ritual was outlawed by the United States government in 1883 under the Bureau of Indian Affairs' Religious Crimes Code, and again in Canada by an 1895 amendment to the Indian Act. It went underground. It is legal again.

**Onbashira**, at the Suwa Taisha shrine in Nagano, Japan, happens every six years. Sixteen fir trees — some nineteen metres long, weighing ten tons — are cut from a sacred mountain, dragged down steep slopes by hand (dangerously, occasionally fatally), and erected in groups of four at the corners of each of the shrine's four buildings. The pillars are *yorishiro*: vessels into which the Shinto gods, the *kami*, descend. The festival has continuity at Suwa across more than twelve centuries.

**Siberian shamanic ascent**, documented by ethnographers across the Altai, Buryat, Evenk, Nanai, and Yakut peoples, erects a birch tree inside the shaman's tent — sometimes passing through the smoke hole — cuts nine notches into its trunk as the steps of the nine heavens, and sends the shaman, dressed in a feathered robe, climbing it in trance.

**Naghol**, or land-diving, on Pentecost Island in Vanuatu, sends men leaping head-first from wooden towers up to thirty metres tall with forest vines tied to their ankles, calibrated so that the diver's hair just brushes the soil at the bottom of the fall. The vines have the right elasticity only in April through June. The ritual is timed to the yam harvest.

**The maypole**, across much of northern and central Europe, is the one everyone has seen. A tall pole erected in a village square, ribbons trailing from its top, dancers weaving the ribbons around it in spring festivals loosely associated with May Day. Popular accounts will tell you it is a pre-Christian fertility survival. Keep a hand on that claim. We come back to it.

**The cult of Cybele** — less famous, but the most historically documented of them all — was an Anatolian religion whose central rite was the cutting and carrying of a sacred pine. The *dendrophori*, literally "the tree-bearers," cut a pine every March, bound an image of the dying god Attis to its trunk, and processed it into the temple. In 204 BCE, during the Second Punic War, the Roman Senate — prompted by the Sibylline books and an embassy to King Attalus I of Pergamon — imported the cult wholesale from Phrygia. Rome kept it, banned parts of it, and watched it outlive the republic.

Seven rituals, five continents, some of them separated by thousands of years. The shape keeps coming back: a pole cut from a tree, a body put at some kind of risk, a descent or ascent or suspension, and a prayer for the ground to keep producing what the community needs to live.

What's going on?

## What Eliade saw

The word that made sense of all of this, for a long time, was *axis mundi*. The phrase is Latin — "the axis of the world" — and it named a pattern the Romanian historian of religion Mircea Eliade spent a career tracing across the world's cosmologies. His books — *Patterns in Comparative Religion* (1958), *The Sacred and the Profane* (1959), and the foundational *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* (French 1951, English 1964) — built the frame that most subsequent thinking about cross-cultural ritual inherits.

Eliade's claim, stated carefully, was this. Archaic societies experience space as charged and uneven. Most of it is undifferentiated, but certain places are *oriented* — places where, once, something divine showed itself. Communities locate themselves in an otherwise unbounded world by planting a ritual marker at such a place: a pole, a tree, a mountain, a ladder, a pillar, a rope of smoke rising through the roof of a tent. The marker is understood as a channel. It joins the three worlds — underworld, earth, sky — into a single continuous line. The divine descends along it; the human ascends. To build a temple, in Eliade's reading, is to re-found the centre. Every shrine is a restatement of the cosmogony.

The pattern, he argued, recurs across traditions that cannot have been in contact. The Sun Dance pole is described by Lakota interpreters — from the inside, in their own theological vocabulary — as the *Tree of Life*, the seam by which prayers reach Wakan Tanka, the Lakota name for the sacred power at the ground of things. The Onbashira pillars are literally called heavenly-pillar stabilisers of creation. The Siberian shaman's birch has nine notches because the upper world is imagined as nine layers. Jacob dreams of a ladder. The Buddhist cosmos is centred on Mount Meru. Islamic tradition has the *mi'raj*, Muhammad's night journey through seven heavens. The Norse hang everything on Yggdrasil. The Voladores caporal faces the four cardinal directions from the top of his pole for the same reason a Tibetan mandala is squared around its central Buddha: the geometry keeps re-appearing.

Eliade's term is good enough that most of us have stopped hearing its strangeness. It describes something. When we sent scouts to ask whether the Voladores had cousins, his framework is what they were reaching for.

But the framework is contested. Jonathan Z. Smith's 1987 book *To Take Place* is the most sustained attack. Smith argued, with care, that Eliade's "universal pattern" flattens genuinely different cosmologies into a single imagined theology, that he treats European comparative categories as if they were features of the world rather than features of the scholar, and that the archaic is not a stable interpretive category at all. Ivan Strenski has said similar things. The recurring shape is real. Whether it amounts to a single underlying *idea* that different cultures are dimly glimpsing — or whether it is a family of different ideas that happen to look similar from where we are standing — is, genuinely, not settled.

Hold that hedge. It matters in a minute.

## The body on the seam

Before we get to the problem Eliade's framework has, one thing the framework is useful for. Once you notice the vertical-pole pattern, you cannot unsee something else running alongside it: a lot of these rituals put a human body at real risk.

The Sun Dancer is pierced and tethered. The Charak devotee swings by hooks through the skin of his back. The Voladores flyer is thirty metres up with a rope at his waist and a knot he trusts to hold. The Vanuatu land-diver's vine is measured to brush his hair against soil and no further, and it does not always work. Naghol has killed people. Sun Dances have.

This is not, I want to suggest, the same thing as asceticism. Asceticism is chronic: the monk's rule, the Stoic's daily exercise, the fast that shapes a life over years. Nor is it the same thing as penance — the wrongdoer paying a debt. The pattern at work in these rituals is acute and intercessory. A body is made temporarily available at high cost. What flows through it is not for the sufferer's own account.

The Lakota holy man Black Elk told his interpreter Joseph Epes Brown in 1953, speaking of the Sun Dance tree: "Here underneath you I shall offer up my body and soul for the sake of the people. For the good of the people it must be done." That is the grammar. Not *I will purify myself*. Not *I will pay for my sins*. *I will make my body available, at real cost, so something can come through it to my community.*

The Voladores flyer is the calibrated-risk version of the same move. He is not trying to die. He is not trying to suffer. But he is staking his body on the ritual's mechanics — on the rope, the knot, the rotation of the wooden frame at the top of the pole — and the stake is what makes the descent an offering rather than a performance. Remove the risk and you don't have a safer ritual. You have a different one. The Bengali hook-swingers told British administrators in 1865 what their descendants have told every reformer since: a ritual without the cost is not the same ritual. It will not do the thing.

This grammar — the body as the fund of last resort, the thing the community turns to when softer offerings are not enough — showed up with enough consistency across the scouting run that our wisdom scout ended up naming it. We've started calling it *body-as-offering*. It is a heuristic, not a theology, but it holds across the cases. And it is distinct, importantly, from the Sufi concept of *fana*, the interior annihilation of the ego in the divine. The two grammars are adjacent. They meet at the limit (Rumi read the execution of al-Hallaj as a fana made visible), but they are not the same move. Fana is interior. Body-as-offering is exterior and transactional. Two grammars in a shared alphabet of devotion.

One pattern across the cases is hard to ignore, and I want to name it before we move on. Almost everywhere the body-as-offering grammar turns up, its practitioners come from communities with the least material protection. The Bengali hook-swingers are overwhelmingly from non-Brahmin agrarian castes. The Plains nations adopted the modern Sun Dance in its current form in the wake of dispossession. The Filipino *penitentes* who have themselves crucified on Good Friday are poor. The people with the least to guard are the most willing to offer what they still have, which is the body. That is not a coincidence. It is also not unrelated to why every empire that has encountered the pattern has tried to ban it.

## The bird at the top

One more thing to notice before the turn.

The Voladores flyers descend from the pole with their arms held straight out at their sides. The Totonac name for the ritual, *kgosni*, means "the flyers." The Spanish is the same: *Danza de los Voladores*, the Dance of the Flyers. Ethnographers describe the body position as deliberately bird-like, and several regional accounts — which one bird the flyer becomes varies by informant — name the descended flyer as an eagle, a macaw, or a quetzal. The bird on top of the axis is a specific image, and it travels.

The Siberian shaman's feathered robe is the first documented version. The shaman, Eliade wrote in 1951, does not dress as a bird for ornament. He *becomes* a bird in order to make the climb to the upper world. Only a bird can travel the vertical seam. The feathers do work; they are not costume.

Twelve hundred years later, in Nishapur, Persia, the Sufi poet Farid ud-Din Attar wrote a long allegorical poem called *The Conference of the Birds* — *Mantiq-ut-Tayr*, composed around 1177. A thousand birds, each representing some human fault that keeps the soul from reaching the divine, gather under the leadership of the hoopoe, who knows the way. They set out to find their king, the Simurgh, a mythical bird whose name in Persian is an unresolvable pun: *si murgh* means "thirty birds." They cross seven valleys — Quest, Love, Knowledge, Detachment, Unity, Wonderment, and Poverty-and-Annihilation. Most of the birds die or turn back. Thirty arrive at the court of the Simurgh and discover the pun: they are what they were looking for. The soul that made the journey *is* the divine it was searching for.

The Egyptian *ba* is drawn as a bird with a human head. Plato's *Phaedrus* says the soul had wings before it was incarnated, and philosophy is the discipline of growing them back. The Norse valkyries are bird-maidens. Christian iconography makes the Holy Spirit a dove. None of this is universal — classical Chinese thought prefers the butterfly, Buddhist sources tend to reach for lamps or flames — but the recurrence is strong enough that when a scholar finds a bird and a vertical axis in the same ritual, they have a legible pattern on their hands.

The Voladores sit at the intersection. The flyer is the body-as-offering on top of the world-pillar, released as a bird. Three patterns meet in a single ritual.

So now we have the question that the scouting run was eventually going to run into.

If the same vertical architecture keeps showing up, and the same body-as-offering grammar keeps showing up, and the same bird-at-the-apex image keeps showing up — how do we know these rituals are not actually related? How do we know we are not looking at a single ancient inheritance, fragmented and scattered across the globe but still legible to the patient eye?

## The maypole problem

Here is where the scouting run turned on itself.

Of all the cousins of the Voladores, the one most Europeans have physically touched at some point in their lives is the maypole. The beribboned pole in the village square on May Day, children weaving patterns around it in a dance that, every popular account will tell you, descends from pre-Christian fertility rites practised by pagan ancestors in some misty prior millennium. The maypole is, in the popular imagination, the axis mundi in its purest village-English form. A pole planted in the earth at the height of spring. A circle of people around it. An obvious descendant of something much older.

Except it isn't.

Ronald Hutton is a historian at the University of Bristol, and in 1996 he published a book called *The Stations of the Sun: A History of the Ritual Year in Britain* that made a careful archival argument. Maypoles, Hutton showed, do not appear in British records until the fourteenth century. The earliest clear attestations are medieval — parish accounts, civic festival records, village celebrations. There is no evidence, Hutton argued, that the people who erected the first documented maypoles understood them as cosmic axes, as pagan survivals, or as anything much beyond a local celebration of spring. The axis-mundi reading of the maypole was imposed retroactively, in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, by comparative mythologists looking for universal patterns and finding them in every pole they came across.

The scholarship Hutton was pushing back against was not sloppy. James Frazer's *The Golden Bough* (first published 1890, expanded over decades) and Wilhelm Mannhardt's earlier German folklore studies were monumental works of synthesis. They were wrong in a specific way. They collapsed distinct village customs — some medieval, some early modern, some almost contemporary — into a single imagined ancient substratum, called it a survival, and used the resulting edifice as evidence for whatever comparative pattern was being assembled. Eliade inherited pieces of this apparatus. When Eliade's readers found maypoles in the European countryside, they knew what they were looking at without needing to check.

This matters, for the Voladores scouting run, in a specific way. If the maypole — the cousin closest to most English-speaking readers — is not actually in the family tree at all, then the whole method of gathering rituals by resemblance has a problem. *Resemblance is not inheritance.* The fact that two rituals look alike does not mean one descended from the other, or that they share a common ancestor, or that they are expressing the same cosmology in different idioms. They may simply both be what you get when you cut down a tall tree and stand it up in a clearing at the start of spring.

Hutton's point is not that Eliade was wrong about everything. Eliade's framework is useful. The Sun Dance pole really is described by Lakota interpreters as the Tree of Life. The Onbashira pillars really are called heavenly-pillar stabilisers. The Voladores count really does line up with the calendar round, or if the count does not quite survive forensic verification, its practitioners still understand the pole as a cosmological axis. The inside-view — what scholars of religion call the *emic* description, the one the practitioners themselves would recognise — does use axis-mundi vocabulary across multiple traditions. Eliade did not invent that.

What Eliade did was extend the inside-view vocabulary past the point at which it was still the inside view. The maypole is the clearest case where the framework ran past the evidence. There are others, Hutton argued: seasonal customs classified as pagan survivals that are transparently medieval civic events, folk rites described as fertility magic that are transparently courtship games, an entire structure of imagined ancient European paganism that looks, on close inspection, like the retroactive projection of nineteenth-century scholarship onto fourteenth-century village life.

The uncomfortable thing about Hutton's argument is that once you take the methodological point seriously, you cannot not take it seriously. Every claim you make about a ritual carrying ancient cosmological meaning is now subject to the same question: is this meaning inside the ritual, or is it on the scholar? Is it emic, or is it imposed?

For the Voladores, the answer is genuinely both at once. Totonac practitioners describe the ritual cosmologically. The pole is understood, locally, as a channel between earth and sky. This is not imposed. But it is also *our* vocabulary — *axis mundi*, world-pillar, cosmic axis — that lets us put the Voladores in the same paragraph as Onbashira and the Sun Dance, and that vocabulary was invented by a twentieth-century Romanian scholar writing in French, and it carries his assumptions along with it.

We can use the word and still be careful about what the word is doing.

## What the scouts actually brought back

Here is the uncomfortable close.

The Voladores has no verified Old World cousins. It has intra-Mesoamerican relatives — most clearly an Aztec pole-climbing rite called *Xocotl Huetzi*, dedicated to the fire god Xiuhtecuhtli, in which youths climbed a decorated pole in a competitive ritual for a figure of the god placed at the top, documented well before Spanish contact. Gertrude Kurath and Samuel Marti's 1964 *Dances of Anáhuac* trace the line from Xocotl Huetzi through Huastec, Nahua, and Otomí intermediaries to the Totonac flyers. That is the one solid family line inside the whole tangle. Everything else on our list — Bengal, the Plains, Vanuatu, Japan, the Siberian birch, the European maypole — is unrelated in any sense a historian would accept.

One small honest asterisk on that claim. Siberian shamanism and the Plains Sun Dance do share something at great depth. The peopling of the Americas from Siberia around fifteen to twenty thousand years ago brought an ancestral population across Beringia into North America, and the continuity of animistic, three-worlds, ecstatic-journey cosmology between Paleo-Siberian traditions and some American ones is real enough that specialists take it seriously as a substrate. Whether *pole ritual specifically* was part of that ancient baggage is plausible but unprovable. Ritual does not fossilise, and fifteen thousand years is a long time for anything to drift. Label the connection "plausible deep-time substrate, not verified transmission" and move on. It does not rescue the idea that the Voladores and the Sun Dance are cousins in any useful sense.

They look alike because the underlying problem is universal. You are a community. You need rain and you need your crops to come in and you need your children to survive to adulthood. You live under a sky that holds the rain and above a ground that holds the crops. You have tall trees, and you have rope, and you have bodies, and you have sound. What you build out of those materials is under-constrained in many ways, but the vertical is not one of them. The vertical is how the human body already experiences the world, and it is where the rain comes from, and it is where your dead are imagined to have gone. If you are going to try to bargain with the sky, the pole you plant is going to go straight up.

It would be astonishing if every continent had not independently invented some version of this ritual. The resemblance, in most cases, is not inheritance. It is geometry and ecology meeting a universal cosmological problem, with the materials at hand.

The maypole does not belong in the family. Most of the other cousins aren't cousins; they are strangers who look alike in a photograph. The Voladores is its own thing, with its own Totonac cosmology, its own Mesoamerican ancestor, and its own calendar arithmetic, and reading it as a variant of a universal sacred pattern — while not entirely wrong — is a move that requires the scholar's hands to be clearly in view.

Three things kept showing up across the run, and it is worth naming them plainly before we stop. A vertical structure joining earth and sky. A body endangered on that structure as the medium of prayer. And a bird at the top — the image of what the soul, briefly, gets to become. You can find all three in Papantla, in the Sun Dance lodge, and in Attar's twelfth-century Persian allegory about birds looking for God. They go together with suspicious regularity. I am not yet ready to say they go together because they are secretly the same idea. I am more inclined to say that the human body, standing on the ground under the sky, keeps asking the same question — and gets handed, by the geometry of the world it finds itself inside, versions of the same three answers.

The sound dimension of all this is its own story. So is what happened to several of these rituals in the 1860s through the 1890s, when a very particular wave of imperial reform tried to end a number of them at once. Those are for another day.

But we can say this much now. The next time someone tells you that a village ritual is a pre-Christian survival, or that the maypole is the axis mundi, or that the Voladores is a variant of some universal sacred pattern — remember Hutton at Bristol, checking the parish records. The pattern is real enough, most of the time, to be worth noticing. And the inheritance, most of the time, is not there to be found.
